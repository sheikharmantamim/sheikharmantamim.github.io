Work Under Review

  • "Poverty reduction and survival: Long-term experimental evidence from Bangladesh"
    with Munshi Sulaiman, Narayan Chandra Das, Imran Matin
    Under submission at Science Advances; Accepted for presentation at the CSAE 2026 Conference, University of Oxford
    Abstract

    Poverty is closely linked to poor health, yet causal evidence on whether reducing poverty improves survival remains limited. We study the long-term health impacts of an Ultra-Poor Graduation (UPG) program, a multifaceted anti-poverty intervention, evaluated through a randomized controlled trial in Bangladesh. Seventeen years after the intervention ended, treatment households experienced significantly lower mortality than control households. Male mortality fell by 14%, with effects appearing early and persisting, while female mortality fell by 17%, emerging only later and concentrated among women aged 55 and above. Survival analysis confirms significantly higher life expectancy for both sexes in treatment households. While multiple pathways may have contributed to these mortality gains, we find evidence for reduced morbidity and improved access to healthcare. Overall morbidity declined by about 10%, with the greatest improvements among elderly women, alongside a minimum 10% increase in healthcare spending among treatment households. These results provide rare experimental evidence that sustained poverty reduction improves adult survival. They underscore the health dividends of integrated anti-poverty programs and suggest that evaluations of such interventions should incorporate health outcomes alongside economic indicators.

  • "The Impact of International Migration on Landholding: Evidence from Bangladesh"
    with Narayan Chandra Das
    Revision and Resubmit in Journal of Development Studies
    Abstract

    This paper estimates the effect of the international migration of low-skilled workers on landholding in the context of Bangladesh. Results show that international migration has a strong negative impact on the amount of owned cultivable land. We also find that international migration decreases the amount of operated land, the total owned land minus rented-out land plus rented-in land. Suggestive evidence shows that the short-run negative effects on landholding intensifies in the long-run. These results suggest that international migration has a distributional effect on landholding. Further analysis indicates that international migration remarkably increases the value of the owned living house, living house ownership, educational expenditures, financial asset, business ownership and capital.

  • "Asset Transfers and Informal Borrowing: Long-run Effects of a Graduation Program in Bangladesh"
    with Narayan Chandra Das and Ashik Sufi Islam
    Abstract

    Research shows that borrowing from informal sources is preferable in many situations because informal lenders are often better informed about the personal circumstances of borrowers or have lower monitoring and enforcement costs. Evidence also shows that informal loans significantly reduce the poverty incidence of borrowers. In this paper, we estimate the effect of an ultra-poor graduation (UPG) program on access to informal loans in the context of Bangladesh. We find that the program decreases informal loans in the short-run, but the long-run effect is positive. Specifically, the amount of informal loans declines by 29% four years after the program support, but seven and 11 years after the intervention, the amount increases by 46% and 36%, respectively. The program also increases formal loans, suggesting that the increase in informal loans is not achieved at the cost of formal loans. Our results suggest that the UPG program helps craft a sustainable graduation pathway for the ultra-poor by increasing access to informal credit in the long-run.
